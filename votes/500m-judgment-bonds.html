June 3, 2025 7-0 Unanimous

Authorization of $500M Judgment Obligation Bonds Sale

View on Legistar

What This Vote Was

On June 3, 2025, the LAUSD board unanimously authorized borrowing up to $500 million to settle sexual abuse claims filed by former students under California Assembly Bill 218. The law, passed in 2019, temporarily removed the statute of limitations for childhood sexual abuse claims against public agencies, allowing survivors to sue for incidents dating back to the 1940s.

The bonds — called judgment obligation bonds — do not require voter approval, unlike school construction bonds. They are repaid directly from LAUSD’s general fund over 15 years, meaning the money comes from the same budget that funds classrooms, teachers, and student services.

The board approved the measure without comment or public presentation. Board members were briefed in small groups beforehand. No public hearing was held. The total cost including interest was estimated at $765 million — a figure the district did not disclose until two weeks after the vote.

Why It Matters

This vote is a window into how LAUSD makes major financial decisions. A commitment of $500 million — eventually costing $765 million with interest — was approved without a single public word of discussion. No board member asked a question. No parent had the chance to comment.

The underlying abuse claims represent a genuine moral obligation to survivors. But the way the vote was handled illustrates a broader pattern: decisions with massive financial consequences for students are made quietly, behind closed doors, without public accountability.

This same general fund — now committed to $765 million in bond repayments — is the one LAUSD cited when saying it couldn’t afford to pay teachers fairly or reduce class sizes. Every dollar committed here is a dollar not available for classrooms.
