About LAUSD Contract Watch

The Los Angeles Unified School District serves over 420,000 students across more than 1,000 schools. Every year, billions of dollars flow from the district to private companies. According to United Teachers Los Angeles, LAUSD has committed $6.7 billion to private multi-year contracts since 2022, including $1.6 billion to outside tech companies. That’s roughly $1 million per school in tech spending alone.

The urgency of this work became undeniable in February 2026 when the FBI raided Superintendent Carvalho’s home and office in connection with a $6.2 million AI contract with a company whose founder was arrested for fraud. Carvalho was placed on paid administrative leave — but many dubious contracts are still in place.

I am a parent of two students in LAUSD. I couldn’t find a place that made LAUSD’s private contracts searchable and understandable, so I made one.

Our Mission

LAUSD Contract Watch exists to make public spending on private contracts searchable and understandable. We believe every dollar sent to a private company should be justified by clear student benefit — and that communities deserve access to the tools to find that information.

What This Site Is

A searchable database of LAUSD contracts, built from publicly available board records. Every contract links to its original source document so anyone can verify the data and flag errors.

The database currently covers contracts approved at board meetings in 2025 — the only records publicly available through LAUSD’s Legistar system. Contracts approved in prior years require a public records request. We filed two. LAUSD did not respond to either.

The site also includes:

  • Analysis of key vendors: ownership structure, red flags, and questions worth asking
  • Board member profiles and vote records sourced from Legistar
  • Five thematic investigations connecting contracts to student outcomes
  • A sources page linking everything to primary documents

This is a starting point for investigation, not a final verdict. We say that often and we mean it.

About the Data

The contract data on this site comes from publicly available LAUSD Board of Education records. Under California’s Public Records Act (Government Code §6250), public agencies are required to make records of government business available to the public. Every contract over $250,000 requires board approval and is documented in board meeting agendas and attachment reports published through LAUSD’s Legistar system — the same platform used by hundreds of local governments nationwide.

Only one year of records is required to be posted online. Any contract that began before 2025 requires a records request. Those requests have been filed. Once we receive that information we will add it to the site.

Information about companies comes from publicly available sources including company websites, news coverage, SEC filings, bankruptcy court records, and corporate databases.

Board member disclosures are based on Form 700 filings — required annually from all California public officials who make or influence government decisions. Board members and senior administrators must disclose their investments, income sources, business positions, and gifts received. These are public documents under California’s Political Reform Act (Government Code §81000).

You can find links to all our sources on the Sources page.

How I Built This

I am a parent of two students at LAUSD who cares deeply about threats to public education from private companies. I wanted the information about how public money is allocated at LAUSD to be easier for people like me to find. I used Claude Code, an AI coding tool, to turn that vision into a working website.

I used it to help find, download, and extract information from the data I needed — a task that would have taken one person months to do manually. I did spot checks to verify the numbers, revised the process when errors were found, and made judgment calls about when the information was accurate enough to publish. I shared the site with others to help find errors and make changes.

Every contract on this site links directly to its original source document so anyone can verify the data themselves and flag errors if they find them.

On Using AI for This

Although I am wary of tech companies using AI to consolidate power and profits, I also believe we are in a moment where these tools can and should be used for the greater good. Whether you are an artist, a writer, or someone who has posted pictures online, these companies have used your material — copyrighted or not — to train their models. Some people choose to boycott these companies for that reason. Personally, I believe that’s even more reason we should be using these tools to help us achieve our goals.

I am also aware of the huge environmental cost of AI data centers. I believe this is a systemic problem that requires systemic solutions. Some countries are already ahead of the US — Iceland and Denmark use data center waste heat to warm homes and buildings. Sweden has data centers that feed into district heating networks. These aren’t futuristic ideas; they’re operating today. The US has been slow to adopt similar approaches, and that’s a policy failure worth fighting. In the meantime, I don’t think the answer is to cede this technology to the corporations and government officials who will use it to consolidate power. Unilateral disarmament helps no one.

What AI is genuinely good at is processing large amounts of publicly available information that is technically accessible but practically impossible for one person to get through. LAUSD has hundreds of board meeting documents, dozens of Form 700 filings, and contracts spanning years. An AI agent can find, download, and extract data from all of it in hours — a task that I, as a working mom, could never have done on my own.

Ultimately, this site is for identifying which contracts might warrant a closer look. It does not decide what action to take, what conclusions to draw, or what is and isn’t wrongdoing. I am using AI to help others decide where to focus our limited time and energy — which public records requests to file, where to ask harder questions, and what to bring to journalists and board meetings.
